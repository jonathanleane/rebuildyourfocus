# Does Dual N-Back Work? A Literature Review

> Dual n-back training reliably improves performance on the trained task itself, and probably produces near transfer to similar n-back variants. Whether it generates meaningful far transfer — to fluid intelligence (Gf) or broad working memory capacity — is genuinely contested. The original [Jaeggi et al. (2008)](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0801268105) paper sparked enormous excitement, and a minority of subsequent studies report modest Gf gains. However, the majority of well-controlled replications, and every major meta-analysis that uses active (rather than passive) control groups, finds no convincing far transfer. The scientific community's working consensus as of 2026 is that dual n-back is not a reliable cognitive enhancer for healthy adults, though it may offer selective benefits for specific populations (e.g., ADHD — see [Núñez Castellar et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.3390/brainsci10100715) and [Guàrdia-Olmos et al. 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12468938/)) and for near-transfer outcomes.

---

## What Dual N-Back Is and What It Claims

The n-back task was introduced by [Kirchner (1958)](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0045137) as a measure of working memory load. In the dual version popularised by Jaeggi et al. ([2003](https://doi.org/10.1080/09658210244000397), [2008](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0801268105)), participants simultaneously monitor two streams — a visual stream (squares appearing at one of eight screen positions) and an auditory stream (spoken consonants) — and must press a key whenever either stimulus matches the one presented *n* steps earlier. The level of *n* adapts continuously to keep the participant at their performance limit, theoretically preventing strategy formation and keeping executive-control demands high throughout training.

The paradigm's theoretical appeal rests on two pillars. First, working memory and fluid intelligence share substantial variance (Engle et al., 1999; Conway et al., 2003), both drawing on attentional control and binding processes in lateral prefrontal and parietal cortex. Second, if the overlap is causal rather than merely correlational, systematically stressing the shared executive machinery through intensive training should elevate general reasoning capacity. [Jaeggi et al. (2008)](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0801268105) operationalised this argument: training a "pure" executive-demand task that resists specific strategies might therefore transfer to Gf tests that have never been practised directly — a prospect with major implications for education and cognitive aging.

## Timeline of Key Studies

**[1958 · Kirchner · Journal of Experimental Psychology](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0045137)**
*Age differences in short-term retention of rapidly changing information.* (n≈14 per group, paradigm paper)
Introduced the n-back paradigm as a laboratory measure of "information load" on memory. No training claims; purely a measurement tool.

**[2003 · Jaeggi, Buschkuehl, Perrig & Meier · Memory](https://doi.org/10.1080/09658210244000397)**
*The concurrent validity of the N-back task as a working memory measure.* (n=~80, validation study)
Established correlations between n-back performance and standard complex-span working memory measures. Noted that the correlation is modest (r ≈ .30), a finding later used by critics.

**[2008 · Jaeggi, Buschkuehl, Jonides & Perrig · PNAS](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0801268105)**
*Improving fluid intelligence with training on working memory.* (n=70, original study)
Four experiments; passive (no-contact) controls; 8–19 sessions of adaptive dual n-back. Trained groups outperformed controls on Raven's Matrices or BOMAT (Cohen's d ≈ 0.65 for the training advantage at posttest). Transfer was dose-dependent: 8 days produced negligible gains, 19 days produced the largest. This paper launched the field.

**[2011 · Jaeggi, Buschkuehl, Jonides & Shah · PNAS](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1103228108)**
*Short- and long-term benefits of cognitive training.* (n=62 children, longitudinal)
Children trained on dual n-back showed Gf gains that persisted at a 3-month follow-up. Effect sizes were similar to 2008. Still used a passive (waitlist) control group, leaving Hawthorne effects uncontrolled.

**[2012 · Chooi & Thompson · Intelligence](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.intell.2012.07.004)**
*Working memory training does not improve intelligence in healthy young adults.* (n=57, null result)
5-week web-based dual n-back (treatment) vs. active control. Repeated-measures MANOVA found no improvements in working memory, processing speed, or fluid intelligence composites. Bayesian analyses supported the null. One of the first well-controlled negative replications.

**[2012 · Shipstead, Redick & Engle · Psychological Bulletin](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0027473)**
*Is working memory training effective?* (review/critique)
Systematic methodological critique identifying four recurring problems: single-task transfer measures, invalid WM tasks, no-contact control groups, and subjective outcome measurement. Argued that the field had not demonstrated that WM *capacity* actually increases; transfer was consistent with skill-specific learning or retest effects.

**[2013 · Redick, Shipstead, Harrison, et al. · JEP: General](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0029342)**
*No evidence of intelligence improvement after working memory training: A randomized, placebo-controlled study.* (n=73, null result)
20 sessions of adaptive dual n-back vs. a matched active control (visual search). Extensive battery of near- and far-transfer measures. No improvement on fluid intelligence (14 Gf measures), working memory, or attentional control. This is the most-cited randomised placebo-controlled refutation of Jaeggi 2008.

**[2013 · Melby-Lervåg & Hulme · Developmental Psychology](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0028228)**
*Is working memory training effective? A meta-analytic review.* (k=23 studies, meta-analysis)
Pooled all published WM training studies (children and adults). Near-transfer to trained WM tasks: d = 0.79 immediately post-training, not maintained at follow-up. Far transfer to Gf: d = 0.19 overall; near zero when restricted to studies with active controls (d ≈ 0.00). First major meta-analysis to show the passive-vs-active-control distinction is the decisive moderator.

**[2013 · Redick & Lindsey · Psychonomic Bulletin & Review](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-013-0453-9)**
*Complex span and n-back measures of working memory: A meta-analysis.* (meta-analysis)
Correlation between complex-span WM tasks and n-back tasks across studies: r ≈ .20 (sharing ~4% variance). This low convergent validity implies that n-back training might not train "working memory" in the sense measured by complex-span tasks — raising doubts about what, exactly, is being trained.

**[2014 · Au, Sheehan, Tsai, Duncan, Buschkuehl & Jaeggi · Psychonomic Bulletin & Review](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-014-0699-x)**
*Improving fluid intelligence with training on working memory: a meta-analysis.* (k=20 studies, meta-analysis)
Identified 20 n-back training studies with Gf outcomes. Overall effect g = 0.24. Argued this effect was robust even in studies with active controls. Critics ([Melby-Lervåg & Hulme, 2015](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-015-0862-z)) contested the study selection criteria and the treatment of baseline differences, arguing the positive effect disappeared under stricter inclusion rules.

**[2014 · Karbach & Verhaeghen · Psychological Science](https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797614548725)**
*Making working memory work: A meta-analysis of executive-control and working memory training in younger and older adults.* (k=61 samples, meta-analysis)
Far-transfer effects were significant when comparing to passive controls but reduced to non-significance versus active controls. Authors cautiously concluded "modest but real" benefits; critics noted the moderator analysis showed effect depended entirely on control type.

**[2015 · Melby-Lervåg & Hulme · Psychonomic Bulletin & Review](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-015-0862-z)**
*There is no convincing evidence that working memory training is effective: A reply to Au et al. (2014) and Karbach and Verhaeghen (2014).* (meta-analytic rebuttal)
Reanalysed Au et al.'s data correcting for baseline imbalances and applying pre-registered inclusion rules. Effect on Gf versus active controls dropped to d ≈ 0.05, non-significant. Also reanalysed Karbach & Verhaeghen: far-transfer effects versus active controls non-significant.

**[2015 · Dougherty, Hamovitz & Tidwell · Psychonomic Bulletin & Review](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-015-0865-9)**
*Reevaluating the effectiveness of n-back training on transfer through the Bayesian lens: Support for the null.* (Bayesian reanalysis)
Reanalysed Au et al.'s dataset using Bayes factors. Studies with passive controls: strong evidence for an effect. Studies with active controls: modest evidence *for the null* (BF favoured null by ~3:1). Concluded the Au et al. effect is largely attributable to Hawthorne/placebo responding.

**[2016 · Melby-Lervåg, Redick & Hulme · Perspectives on Psychological Science](https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691616635612)**
*Working memory training does not improve performance on measures of intelligence or other measures of "far transfer": Evidence from a meta-analytic review.* (k=87 studies, 145 comparisons, definitive meta-analysis)
Largest and most systematic meta-analysis to date. Near-transfer (WM tasks): d = 0.43, reliable. Far-transfer (Gf, verbal ability, word reading, arithmetic): d = 0.11–0.19 overall; versus treated controls, near zero and non-significant for all categories. Training effects did not persist at follow-up for far-transfer outcomes.

**[2016 · Stephenson & Halpern · PLOS ONE](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0151817)**
*Dual N-Back Working Memory Training in Healthy Adults: A Randomized Comparison to Processing Speed Training.* (n=136, null result, active control)
Randomly assigned healthy adults to adaptive dual n-back or processing-speed training (both 20 sessions). No group differences on WM, processing speed, or fluid intelligence composites. Supports null hypothesis via Bayesian follow-up analyses.

**[2017 · Soveri, Antfolk, Karlsson, Salo & Laine · Psychonomic Bulletin & Review](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-016-1217-0)**
*Working memory training revisited: A multi-level meta-analysis of n-back training studies.* (k=33 studies, 203 effect sizes, meta-analysis)
Applied multi-level modelling to account for dependencies. Medium transfer to untrained n-back variants (near transfer), but very small effects for other WM tasks (d ≈ 0.18–0.37), fluid intelligence (d ≈ 0.18), and cognitive control. No significant moderating effects of age, training dose, or single vs. dual n-back format. Concluded that n-back training improves n-back performance, not general cognition.

**[2020 · Núñez Castellar, Notebaert, et al. · Brain Sciences — ADHD](https://doi.org/10.3390/brainsci10100715)**
*Attention networks in ADHD adults after working memory training with a dual n-back task.* (n=~90, ADHD-specific RCT)
Compared adaptive dual n-back to fixed 1-back control in ADHD and neurotypical adults over 20 sessions. Adaptive training improved conflict-attention effects in the ADHD group but not neurotypical controls, and did not affect alerting or orienting. Suggests targeted benefits may exist for ADHD populations.

**[2021 · Heinzel, Rimpel, Stelzel & Rapp · Scientific Reports](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-82663-w)**
*Dual n-back working memory training evinces superior transfer effects compared to the method of loci.* (n=163, active comparison)
Compared dual n-back to method-of-loci (memory strategy) training. Both improved WM-adjacent tasks; dual n-back showed marginally broader near transfer. No Gf outcomes measured, limiting conclusions about far transfer.

**[2025 · Guàrdia-Olmos, Turbau-Terré, et al. · Brain Sciences — ADHD](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12468938/)**
*Boosting working memory in ADHD: Adaptive dual n-back training enhances WAIS-IV performance, but yields mixed Corsi outcomes.* (n=106 [33 non-medicated ADHD, 42 medicated ADHD, 45 controls], RCT)
18+ sessions of adaptive dual n-back vs. fixed 1-back. WAIS-IV Working Memory Index improved significantly in the non-medicated ADHD group (verbal WM benefit); visuospatial WM (Corsi blocks) showed inconsistent transfer. Neurotypical adults showed no significant WM improvement. Reinforces the view that ADHD populations may be a meaningful exception. ([DOI: 10.3390/brainsci15090998](https://doi.org/10.3390/brainsci15090998))

**[2025 · Preregistered double-blind RCT · Acta Psychologica](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.actpsy.2025.104782)**
*Near and far transfer effects of working memory training: A preregistered, double-blind, randomized-controlled study.*
Visuospatial adaptive n-back vs. matched active control in healthy adults. Greater training-session gain in the n-back task with more sessions, but no near or far transfer to untrained cognitive tasks. Authors conclude that even with extended training duration, transfer to other cognitive abilities is not reliably produced.

## Evidence the Paradigm Works

The strongest evidence for dual n-back comes from three sources.

**The original [Jaeggi 2008](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0801268105) signal.** Across four sub-experiments (total n=70), trained participants showed dose-dependent Gf gains unrelated to pre-existing ability level or simple WM capacity (ruling out regression to the mean). Effect sizes at 17–19 days were d ≈ 0.65–0.70. The paper is methodologically transparent and the effect was dose-responsive, which is a hallmark of genuine learning.

**[Au et al. 2014](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-014-0699-x) meta-analysis.** Pooling 20 studies yielded g = 0.24 for Gf, a small but statistically reliable effect. Even restricting to active-control studies, Au et al. argued a positive effect survived. This meta-analysis remains the primary quantitative case for the "works" side.

**Population-specific findings.** The 2020 ([Núñez Castellar et al.](https://doi.org/10.3390/brainsci10100715)) and 2025 ([Guàrdia-Olmos et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12468938/)) ADHD RCTs suggest that people with working memory deficits — ADHD adults — may obtain genuine verbal WM improvements from dual n-back. Attention-conflict (executive control) effects also improved in ADHD groups. This is theoretically plausible: training a deficit may be more effective than training a near-ceiling ability.

**Near transfer is real.** All meta-analyses agree: dual n-back reliably improves performance on other n-back variants. [Soveri et al. (2017)](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-016-1217-0) reported a medium effect (d ≈ 0.5–0.6) for near transfer. If a clinician or researcher wants to measure n-back performance, training on dual n-back works well.

## Evidence It Doesn't (or Barely) Works

The weight of controlled evidence is negative for far transfer.

**The passive-vs-active-control fissure.** This is the single most important empirical finding in the debate. [Melby-Lervåg & Hulme (2013)](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0028228) first showed it; [Melby-Lervåg, Redick & Hulme (2016)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691616635612) confirmed it across 87 studies: Gf effects evaporate when the control group also does *something* (active control) versus sitting at home (passive control). The passive-control Gf effect in the 2016 meta-analysis was d ≈ 0.32; the active-control Gf effect was d ≈ 0.06, non-significant. This pattern is the signature of a Hawthorne/placebo effect rather than genuine cognitive enhancement.

**[Redick et al. (2013)](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0029342).** The most rigorous single-study refutation: randomised, placebo-controlled, 14 Gf measures, active control group, n=73. No effect on any outcome. Importantly, the training group improved as much on the n-back as in positive studies — they just didn't transfer.

**[Chooi & Thompson (2012)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.intell.2012.07.004).** An independent null result with active controls and Bayesian support for the null hypothesis in healthy young adults.

**[Stephenson & Halpern (2016)](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0151817).** n=136 randomised comparison to active processing-speed control; null across all composites.

**The [2025 preregistered double-blind RCT](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.actpsy.2025.104782)** (the most methodologically rigorous study to date) found no near or far transfer in healthy adults despite extended training and high statistical power.

**[Dougherty et al. (2015)](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-015-0865-9) Bayesian reanalysis.** Bayes factors for active-control studies in the Au dataset modestly favoured the null. This is not strong evidence for absence, but it is not reassuring for the "works" position.

**[Soveri et al. (2017)](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-016-1217-0)** found d ≈ 0.18 for Gf across all conditions — a very small effect that likely shrinks further under active-control-only restriction, and that falls within what would be expected from test familiarity or non-specific arousal effects.

## Methodological Controversies

**Active versus passive controls.** The most fundamental issue. Passive controls (no training, sometimes called no-contact or waitlist groups) cannot separate the specific cognitive effect of dual n-back from non-specific effects: motivation, time-on-task, expectation of improvement ("Hawthorne effect"), test familiarity, and experimenter attention. Early positive studies (including [Jaeggi 2008](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0801268105) and [2011](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1103228108)) used passive controls exclusively. Critics argue — with strong support from the [2013](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0028228), [2015](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-015-0862-z), and [2016](https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691616635612) meta-analyses — that this design inflates effect sizes substantially.

**Construct validity of the n-back task.** [Redick & Lindsey (2013)](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-013-0453-9) found that n-back scores and complex-span WM scores share only ~4% of variance. If n-back does not measure working memory in the same sense as complex-span tasks (the primary construct linked to Gf in the literature), then training n-back performance may improve a different, narrower ability. The 2025 paper by Burgoyne et al. found that once measurement error is partialled out, the latent correlation is r ≈ 0.69 — more encouraging, but the debate continues.

**Transfer measure breadth.** Many early positive studies used a single Gf test (e.g., Ravens APM) and a single WM test. [Redick et al. (2013)](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0029342) showed that with a comprehensive battery of 14 Gf measures, no effect survives. [Melby-Lervåg et al. (2016)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691616635612) confirmed that effect size shrinks as the battery grows, consistent with task-specific learning inflating scores on any single measure.

**n-back specificity vs. general WM training.** Near transfer (to other n-back variants) is substantially larger than far transfer. This suggests training is largely task-specific — improving the particular strategies and neural patterns for the n-back format — rather than improving general working memory capacity.

**Dose-response effects.** [Jaeggi (2008)](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0801268105) demonstrated dose-dependence as a positive finding, but no study has systematically identified a threshold beyond which far transfer reliably appears. Most training protocols run 15–20 sessions; longer protocols do not reliably produce larger transfer ([Soveri et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-016-1217-0) found no dose moderator).

**Individual differences.** Some evidence suggests that people with initially lower WM capacity benefit more (consistent with a "floor effect" driving gains). The ADHD literature — [Núñez Castellar et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.3390/brainsci10100715) and [Guàrdia-Olmos et al. (2025)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12468938/) — is the strongest evidence for population-specific benefits. Age may also moderate effects, though results in older adults are mixed; the training effect itself may be smaller in older populations (Matysiak et al., 2019).

**Publication bias.** The field has been subject to typical positive-result publication bias. Meta-analyses applying trim-and-fill or funnel-plot correction tend to reduce effect estimates. The [2015 Melby-Lervåg & Hulme reanalysis](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-015-0862-z) noted that the Au et al. meta-analysis may have been affected by selective inclusion.

## Where the Field Stands Today (as of 2026)

There is no longer serious scientific dispute about the core outcome: dual n-back training robustly improves n-back performance, and that improvement does not reliably transfer to fluid intelligence or broader working memory capacity in healthy adults under well-controlled conditions.

The [2016 Melby-Lervåg, Redick & Hulme meta-analysis](https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691616635612) (the largest and most carefully controlled to date, 87 studies) is the current reference standard. Its main conclusion — that "further attempts to increase working memory capacity by repetitively practicing simple memory tasks on a computer are unlikely to lead to generalized cognitive benefits" — has been corroborated by the [2025 preregistered double-blind RCT](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.actpsy.2025.104782). The [Au et al. (2014)](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-014-0699-x) meta-analysis remains the primary quantitative dissent, with an overall g = 0.24, but this effect is contested, attenuated under active-control analysis, and possibly inflated by baseline imbalances and selective inclusion.

Two active research directions still have genuine scientific interest:

1. **ADHD and clinical populations.** The 2020 ([Núñez Castellar et al.](https://doi.org/10.3390/brainsci10100715)) and 2025 ([Guàrdia-Olmos et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12468938/)) ADHD RCTs suggest that individuals with working memory deficits may obtain verbal WM gains. This is conceptually distinct from the "general cognitive enhancement" claim; it is closer to remediation of a specific deficit.

2. **Neural mechanisms and plasticity markers.** Neuroimaging studies (e.g., Salminen et al., 2016, *Journal of Neuroscience*, striatal activation changes predicting transfer) suggest that individual differences in neural plasticity may mediate who, if anyone, benefits. This line of work is scientifically interesting but has not yet identified trainable predictors of response.

The field has moved from "dual n-back improves intelligence" (2008–2012 consensus) through "highly contested" (2012–2016) to a current position best described as: "no reliable far-transfer benefit for healthy adults; modest near-transfer; possible domain-specific benefit for ADHD."

---

## What This Means for Someone Training

If you are a healthy adult hoping to increase your fluid intelligence through dual n-back training, the honest expectation based on current evidence is that you will get better at the n-back task itself (this is certain and happens quickly), but you are unlikely to see measurable gains in Gf, broad working memory, or real-world cognitive performance. The best-case effect size for Gf in the most favourable meta-analysis ([Au et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-014-0699-x)) is g = 0.24 — roughly equivalent to moving from the 50th to the 60th percentile of Gf — and even this estimate is contested. Most active-control studies find g near zero for Gf. If you have ADHD and significant working memory deficits, there is more (though not definitive) reason to try: recent RCTs ([Núñez Castellar 2020](https://doi.org/10.3390/brainsci10100715), [Guàrdia-Olmos 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12468938/)) suggest verbal WM may improve, and attention-conflict effects may benefit. For anyone training, 15–20 daily sessions of 20–25 minutes is the most commonly studied dose; there is no evidence that longer training reliably produces larger far-transfer gains. What you should *not* expect: lasting IQ improvements, better exam scores, reduced cognitive aging, or meaningful everyday memory benefits. The dual n-back task is cognitively demanding and engaging, which has its own motivational value, but as a brain-training investment its expected return on general cognition is small to negligible by current scientific standards.
